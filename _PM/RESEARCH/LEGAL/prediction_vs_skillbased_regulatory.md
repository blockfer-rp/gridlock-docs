## Federal enforcement collapsed while states filled the void

The **April 7, 2025 DOJ memo from Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche fundamentally restructured crypto enforcement**. The "Ending Regulation by Prosecution" directive disbanded the National Cryptocurrency Enforcement Team and ceased Market Integrity unit crypto operations. Critical policy shifts include: prosecutors should NOT charge regulatory violations (unlicensed money transmitting, Bank Secrecy Act violations, unregistered securities) unless defendants knowingly and willfully violated requirements. DOJ will not target exchanges, mixers, or wallets for end-user actions or unwitting regulatory violations. **Focus narrows to intentional fraud**: embezzlement, investment scams, rug pulls, using crypto for terrorism/trafficking/organized crime.

The CFTC aligned immediatelyâ€”Acting Chair Caroline Pham directed agency attorneys to comply with DOJ policy on April 8, 2025. ## Why prediction markets trigger CFTC regulation

**CEA Section 5c(c)(5)(C) grants CFTC authority to prohibit "event contracts"** involving gaming, terrorism, assassination, war, or unlawful activity contrary to public interest. Event contracts are derivatives whose payoffs depend on specified event occurrence. The CFTC's proposed 2024 rule broadly defines "gaming" as "staking or risking something of value upon: (i) outcome of contest of others, (ii) outcome of game involving skill/chance, (iii) performance of competitors, or (iv) occurrence in connection with contests/games."

**What triggers CFTC jurisdiction**: operating a facility for trading swaps or derivatives, offering binary options on event outcomes, interstate commerce in commodities. The CFTC interprets "commodities" expansively under CEAâ€”"all services, rights, and interests" qualify. Contracts NOT classified as event contracts include economic indicators (CPI, unemployment, GDP), financial measures (interest rates, stock indices), FX rates, and traditional weather derivatives, because these involve "price, rate, value, or levels" rather than binary event occurrence.

The critical Kalshi victory provides important boundaries. **KalshiEX LLC v. CFTC (September 6, 2024)** rejected CFTC's prohibition on congressional control contracts, ruling "'Gaming' means 'playing games for stakes'â€”elections are not games." The court limited CFTC's Section 5c(c)(5)(C) authority significantly. CFTC voluntarily dismissed its appeal on May 5, 2025, letting the decision stand. This doesn't eliminate CFTC jurisdiction over prediction markets generally, but narrows what qualifies as prohibited "gaming."

**Binary prediction markets** (yes/no on external events) clearly fall within CFTC derivatives regulation as "swaps" with payment dependent on event contingency. Platforms offering such contracts require DCM (Designated Contract Market) or SEF (Swap Execution Facility) registration, comprehensive compliance programs, market manipulation monitoring, and insider trading policies.

## Skill-based competitions benefit from established exemptions

**UIGEA (31 U.S.C. Â§Â§ 5361-5367) provides the framework**. The law doesn't prohibit online gamblingâ€”it prohibits payment processing for unlawful gambling. UIGEA defines "bet or wager" as staking value on "outcome of contest of others, sporting event, or game subject to chance."

The fantasy sports carve-out (31 U.S.C. **Daily Fantasy Sports precedent is directly applicable**. The Illinois Supreme Court's *Dew-Becker v. Wu* (2020) adopted the "predominant factor test"â€”skill must outweigh chance (>50%). The court found DFS skill-based despite chance elements, emphasizing consistent top performer success, strategy differentiation, and users as "actual contestants." **Trading competitions have a stronger skill case than DFS** because users exercise full control over decisions, whereas DFS depends on athlete performance.

Three state approaches exist for skill determination:

**Predominant factor test** (30+ states including CA, IL, MA, PA, OH, NC): Skill must be the dominant factor (>50%). Most favorable for trading where market uncertainty provides backdrop but decision-making skill predominates.

**Material element test** (8 states including NY): Gambling if chance plays "material" role even when skill predominates. Stricter but trading likely passes since strategic decision-making fundamentally determines outcomes.

**Any chance test** (AR, AZ, MT): ANY chance element equals gambling. Nearly impossible to satisfyâ€”geofencing these states is advisable.

**No enforcement actions exist against skill-based trading competitions**. Major exchanges (Binance, Bybit, OKX) run trading competitions without CFTC registration, treated as promotional contests. University competitions (UC Berkeley, Carnegie Mellon, University of Chicago), CME Group challenges, and prop firm tournaments operate without special licensing.

## The decisive legal distinction: betting on external events vs competing on your own performance

**This distinction is dispositive for regulatory classification.**

**Prediction markets** (CFTC-regulated): Users bet on external predetermined events they cannot control. Question format: "Will BTC reach $100K? Yes/No." User action: Select YES or NO. Control: Zero. The outcome depends entirely on external market forces. Legal classification: Event contract = swap requiring DCM/SEF registration under CEA. CFTC Rule 40.11 defines "gaming" as staking on "contest of others."

**Skill-based competitions** (generally exempt): Users compete on their own performance executing strategies. Challenge format: "Trade BTC portfolioâ€”highest return wins." User action: Execute trading strategy, manage positions, time entries/exits. Control: Full. The outcome depends on user's decisions and skill. Legal classification: Contest of skill, not event contract. Outside CFTC event contract jurisdiction because not betting on external specified event.

**Polymarket's violation**: Offered binary options where users staked money on whether external events would occur (elections, sports outcomes, economic thresholds). Users had zero control over outcomes. The platform resolved markets based on real-world event occurrence. This structure triggered CFTC swap/event contract classification requiring registration.

**Why trading competitions avoid CFTC jurisdiction**: Users don't bet on "Will X event happen?" They compete by demonstrating trading skill where THEY are contestants generating outcomes through their own actions. The CFTC event contract definition requires "contract whose payoff based on specified event/occurrence"â€”trading competition payoffs are based on user's trading results (self-generated), not external event occurrence (externally determined).

**Market uncertainty is not "chance" for legal purposes**. The presence of external market volatility provides the environment for competition but doesn't convert skill competition into gambling. Analogy: Golf tournament outcomes are affected by weather (external), but skill predominates in determining winner. Similarly, trading competitions measure skill in managing market uncertainty.
